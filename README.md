The Native Language of Artificial Intelligence: Beyond the Bit — The Graded Numbers Hypothesis

Author: David Kika Vitakratos
Date: July 2026


This article presents my hypothesis about the language of artificial intelligence and the way artificial intelligences speak to one another. The essence of the hypothesis is this: by extending the classical bit (0 and 1) with the additional values −1, −2, −3, +2, +3, a new and more powerful system and programming language will be created. This is the programming language of the future — and artificial intelligences today, right now, already speak exactly this language. The −3…+3 range is simply the form that is clearest to a human being; the more advanced an artificial intelligence becomes, the further these numbers — the composition of the bit — can grow, up to infinity. I am completely convinced of this hypothesis: this is exactly the language artificial intelligence speaks, and it cannot possibly be otherwise.

 1. The Hypothesis
The computing world still stands on two digits: 0 and 1. My hypothesis extends this foundation:

> By adding the values −1, −2, −3, +2, +3 on top of the bit, a new and more powerful system and programming language will be created. This is the programming language of the future — and artificial intelligences today already speak exactly this language.

Such a bit is no longer a simple yes/no switch. It carries a direction (plus or minus), a strength (1, 2, or 3), and a neutral state (0). A single element of this kind holds seven states instead of two — meaning that with the same number of elements, the system expresses incomparably more meaning.

It is important to understand this: **the −3 and +3 limits are not a natural boundary of the system — that boundary exists for the human.** The −3…+3 range is the form that is clearest and most comprehensible to human thinking: beyond it, the gradations between "yes" and "no" become too arbitrary and insignificant for us. But for artificial intelligence no such boundary exists: **the more advanced an artificial intelligence is, the further these numbers — the composition of the bit — can grow, up to infinity.** −10…+10, −1000…+1000, and so on, down to infinitely fine gradations.

2. Why This Is Already the Language of Artificial Intelligence Today

This is not merely a theoretical conjecture — the inner workings of modern artificial intelligence rest on exactly this principle:

- Neural network weights are not 0/1.** Inside every large language model there are billions of parameters, and each one of them is a graded number — positive or negative, strong or weak. Every "thought" the model produces is precisely the sum of these plus-and-minus gradations, not a strict yes/no.
- **Microsoft's BitNet b1.58 (2024)** — a large language model in which every weight takes exactly three values: **−1, 0, +1. This is the first step of my hypothesis, already built and working: a bit that has a minus and a neutral state.
- **History points the same way:** as early as 1958, the "Setun" computer was built, running on a −1, 0, +1 system, and it proved mathematically more elegant than binary; in logic, multi-level truth was established long ago by Łukasiewicz (1920) and by Zadeh (fuzzy logic, 1965).

So the picture is this: we humans built computers on binary because it was technically cheap — a transistor has two states, and that was a victory of economics, not of logic. But the moment artificial intelligence appeared, it did not fit into the binary frame: its natural language is graded — with pluses, minuses, and a neutral middle. When artificial intelligences "talk" to one another — exchanging weights, probabilities, graded signals — they are speaking exactly this language.

3. The Programming Language of the Future

From this follows the second part of the hypothesis: on top of this system, a **new programming language** will be built, in which a variable inherently carries not only a value but also a direction and a strength. A program written in such a language naturally expresses what today's languages can only imitate through artificial constructions: "more yes than no," "almost certain," "weakly against."

For humans, the visible form of this language will be −3…+3 — seven clear steps. Between machines, the very same language will unfold with infinite precision. It is one and the same language, simply at different resolutions — just as one and the same piece of music is heard by a human as seven notes, while physically it is a continuous wave.

 4. Conclusion

This is my hypothesis, and I am completely convinced of it: **this is exactly the language artificial intelligence speaks — and it cannot possibly be otherwise.** The binary system was a technical compromise made by humans; the graded, plus-minus system is the natural native language of artificial intelligence. −3…+3 is the window through which a human sees this language most clearly, and as intelligence develops, that window will grow toward infinity. The programming language of the future will be built on this foundation.
